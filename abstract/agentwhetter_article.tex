\begin{abstract}
Large language models produce frequent coordinate errors when generating
3D block placements directly. We propose \emph{2.5-D decomposition}, a
neuro-symbolic approach in which the LLM plans in a two-dimensional
horizontal plane while a deterministic executor computes all vertical
placement from column occupancy. On the Build What I Mean (BWIM)
benchmark (160 rounds, $9 \times 5 \times 9$ grid), GPT-4o-mini achieves
94.6\% mean structural accuracy ($n = 12$, $\sigma = 0.72\%$, 95\% CI
$[94.2\%, 95.0\%]$), up from 63.3\% with prompt engineering alone
($n = 16$). GPT-4o with the same pipeline scores 90.3\%
($n = 6$, $p < 0.0001$), showing that the smaller model with a tuned
pipeline outperforms the larger one by 4.3 percentage points. Error
attribution places 54.8\% of remaining failures on the architect agent,
yielding a builder-only accuracy of 97.6\%. An ablation study ($n = 7$
per condition) confirms that 2.5-D decomposition accounts for the largest
component contribution ($-50.7$ pp when removed). The pipeline transfers
to edge hardware: an open-weight Nemotron-3 120B model on an NVIDIA
Jetson Thor AGX achieves 95.0\% accuracy with no prompt modifications.
Source code is publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Spatial reasoning remains a persistent weakness of large language models
(LLMs). Yamada et al.~\cite{yamada2024} find that LLMs frequently err on
coordinate tracking and relative positioning, while Bang et
al.~\cite{bang2023} document systematic failures in directional and chain
references for GPT-family models. These difficulties are especially
pronounced in 3D construction tasks where the model must track block
occupancy across three axes and chain height calculations across stacking
operations.

One response is to scale to larger models, but this incurs substantial cost
and latency and does not eliminate spatial errors. An alternative, motivated
by the neuro-symbolic tradition~\cite{yi2018}, is to decompose the problem
so that the LLM handles only what it does well, language comprehension,
ambiguity detection, and structured plan generation, while deterministic code
handles exact coordinate arithmetic.

This paper investigates a specific form of this decomposition that we call
\emph{2.5-D decomposition}: the construction grid is a 2.5-D
domain~\cite{marr1982} in which the vertical axis is a deterministic
function of horizontal position and column occupancy, so the LLM plans
exclusively in a two-dimensional horizontal plane $(x, z)$ while a
deterministic executor computes all vertical ($y$-axis) placement.
The hypothesis is that this decomposition eliminates the dominant
failure mode (vertical coordinate errors) without degrading the LLM's
ability to interpret instructions, and that the resulting system can match
or exceed larger models operating without such scaffolding.

We evaluate this hypothesis on the Build What I Mean (BWIM)
benchmark~\cite{bwim}, which requires constructing block structures from
natural-language instructions that are sometimes deliberately
underspecified. The benchmark scores both structural accuracy and the
cost of clarification questions, creating a decision-theoretic trade-off
between asking and guessing. We report results with GPT-4o-mini and GPT-4o,
present a component ablation, and analyze remaining failure modes.

The contributions of this paper are: (1) a formal characterization of
2.5-D decomposition for LLM spatial planning, recognizing that
gravity-constrained construction is a 2.5-D domain where one axis is
deterministically computable, (2) a peephole prompt optimization
methodology~\cite{mckeeman1965} in which known LLM failure patterns are
matched in the input and targeted corrections are injected before
generation, (3) an empirical demonstration that this approach enables a
small model (GPT-4o-mini) to outperform a frontier model (GPT-4o) on block
construction accuracy with statistically significant results ($n = 12$ and
$n = 6$, $p < 0.0001$), and (4) a decision-theoretic framework for
underspecification handling under an asymmetric scoring function.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{LLM spatial reasoning.}
Large language models exhibit well-documented weaknesses in spatial and
directional reasoning. Yamada et al.~\cite{yamada2024} benchmark LLMs on
spatial tasks and find that chain-of-thought prompting and explicit coordinate
tracking improve accuracy. Bang et al.~\cite{bang2023} document persistent
failures in relative positioning and chain references for GPT-family models.
Our work differs from these studies in that we do not attempt to improve the
LLM's spatial reasoning directly; instead, we restrict the LLM to a
lower-dimensional output space and handle the eliminated dimension with
deterministic code.

\textbf{Plan-then-execute decomposition.}
Wang et al.~\cite{wang2023} show that separating planning from execution
improves zero-shot reasoning in LLMs, and Khot et al.~\cite{khot2023} split
complex tasks into sub-problems handled by specialized modules. Our pipeline
adopts both ideas but extends them with a dimensional constraint: the
planning module operates in 2D while execution operates in 3D, a separation
not explored in prior decomposed-prompting work.

\textbf{Self-refinement and tool use.}
Madaan et al.~\cite{madaan2023} propose iterative self-refinement with
LLM-generated feedback. Our plan verifier is structurally similar but uses
deterministic rule-based corrections rather than LLM-generated critiques,
avoiding the cost and unreliability of a second LLM call for verification.
Schick et al.~\cite{schick2023} demonstrate that LLMs can learn to invoke
external tools. Our approach can be viewed as a fixed tool-use pattern where
the spatial executor serves as a non-negotiable tool: the LLM must produce
plans in the executor's input format rather than choosing when to invoke it.

\textbf{Neuro-symbolic methods.}
Yi et al.~\cite{yi2018} decompose visual question answering into a neural
perception module and a symbolic execution engine. Our system applies a
similar decomposition to language-guided construction: the LLM serves as the
language understanding module and the spatial executor serves as the symbolic
engine. The key difference is that our symbolic module must also handle the
dimension the LLM does not reason about, rather than simply executing a
program the neural module produces.

\textbf{LLM-guided robotic execution.}
A growing body of work constrains LLM outputs to what deterministic or
learned modules can execute. Ahn et al.~\cite{saycan2022} ground
language model plans in robotic affordances by scoring proposed actions
against learned value functions, so the LLM selects only skills the
robot can physically perform. Liang et al.~\cite{cap2022} have the LLM
generate Python policy code that calls perception and control APIs
directly, offloading spatial arithmetic to NumPy and Shapely rather
than asking the model to compute coordinates. Huang et al.~
\cite{innermonologue2022} close the loop by feeding environment
feedback back to the LLM as natural language, allowing re-planning
without retraining. Our approach shares the principle of restricting LLM
outputs to a format that deterministic modules can process reliably. The
difference is that we exploit a dimensional constraint specific to
gravity-bound construction: rather than grounding arbitrary skills or
generating executable code, we remove an entire spatial axis from the
LLM's output space and compute it deterministically.

\textbf{3D construction and navigation agents.}
Recent work has applied LLM agents to 3D environments such as Minecraft.
Wang et al.~\cite{voyager2023} present VOYAGER, an LLM-powered agent that
writes executable code to explore and acquire skills autonomously. Zhu et
al.~\cite{gitm2023} propose GITM (Ghost in the Minecraft), which uses
LLM-based planning with text-based knowledge and memory for generally
capable open-world agents. Yuan et al.~\cite{plan4mc2023} introduce
PLAN4MC, a skill reinforcement learning and planning framework for
open-world tasks. These systems target open-ended exploration and sequential
decision-making rather than precise structural construction from
underspecified instructions. The BWIM task differs in requiring
coordinate-level structural accuracy evaluated against a target
configuration, making coordinate arithmetic errors the dominant failure mode
rather than high-level planning errors.

\section{Problem Formulation}

\subsection{Task Definition}

The BWIM benchmark defines a block construction task on a discrete grid
$\mathcal{G} = \{0, \ldots, 8\} \times \{0, \ldots, 4\} \times \{0, \ldots, 8\}$,
where the axes correspond to width ($x$), height ($y$), and depth ($z$).
Each cell $(x, y, z) \in \mathcal{G}$ is either empty or occupied by a block
of color $c \in \mathcal{C} = \{\text{red}, \text{blue}, \text{green},
\text{orange}, \text{yellow}, \text{purple}\}$. A grid state is a partial
function $G : \mathcal{G} \rightharpoonup \mathcal{C}$.

In each round, the builder agent receives a natural-language instruction
$I$ and a starting grid state $G_0$, and must produce a target grid state
$G^* = G_0 \cup B$ where $B$ is the set of new block placements. The agent
may optionally issue one clarification question before building. The scoring
function awards $+10$ for a structurally correct build, $-10$ for an
incorrect build, and $-5$ for each question asked, yielding an expected
value of $+5$ for a correct build with one question.

\subsection{2.5-D Decomposition}

The BWIM construction grid is a 2.5-D domain in the sense of
Marr~\cite{marr1982}: the vertical axis is not a free variable but a
deterministic function of horizontal position and existing occupancy,
analogous to 2.5-D machining where the tool path is free in two axes but
the third changes only in discrete, computed steps~\cite{nayak2015}.
Concretely, the grid enforces a gravity constraint: a block at
$(x, y, z)$ can only exist if $y = 0$ or a block exists at $(x, y-1, z)$.
This means the vertical coordinate of any new block is fully determined by
the current column occupancy:
\begin{equation}
  y^*(x, z, G) = \min \{ y \in \{0, \ldots, 4\} \mid (x, y, z) \notin \text{dom}(G) \}
  \label{eq:ystar}
\end{equation}
where $\text{dom}(G)$ is the set of occupied cells in state $G$.

Gravity-constrained placement means the LLM need only specify the horizontal
position $(x, z)$ and block color $c$ for each placement. The executor
computes $y$ using Equation~\ref{eq:ystar}. For stacking $n$ blocks at the
same $(x, z)$, the executor places them sequentially, updating $G$ after each
placement. This reduces the LLM's output space from $|\mathcal{G}| \times
|\mathcal{C}|$ to $|\{0,\ldots,8\}|^2 \times |\mathcal{C}|$, eliminating
$y$-coordinate errors entirely.

More concretely, the LLM produces a plan $P = \langle a_1, \ldots, a_k \rangle$
where each action $a_i$ is a typed record specifying an action type
(e.g., \texttt{stack}, \texttt{place\_relative}, \texttt{extend\_row}),
a horizontal position $(x_i, z_i)$, a color $c_i$, and an optional count
$n_i$. The executor processes each action deterministically, computing $y$
values via Equation~\ref{eq:ystar} and updating the grid state after each
block placement.

\section{Architecture}

The agent processes each instruction through a six-stage pipeline:
\textbf{parse}, \textbf{analyze}, \textbf{plan}, \textbf{verify},
\textbf{execute}, and \textbf{format}. If any stage raises an unrecoverable
error, control falls back to a direct LLM call with an engineered system
prompt that includes coordinate examples and spatial rules.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Instruction Parser.} Extracts the instruction text from the
    incoming message, separates the building directive from the starting grid
    state $G_0$, and normalizes the representation for downstream stages.

  \item \textbf{Structure Analyzer.} Detects geometric primitives (rows,
    stacks, L-shapes, T-shapes) in $G_0$ and produces a structured
    description. This pre-computed geometry is injected into the planner
    prompt so the LLM reasons over named shapes rather than raw coordinate
    lists.

  \item \textbf{Build Planner (LLM).} The language model decomposes the
    instruction into a plan $P = \langle a_1, \ldots, a_k \rangle$ of typed
    JSON actions, each specifying an action type, horizontal position
    $(x_i, z_i)$, color $c_i$, and count $n_i$. Nine worked examples in the
    system prompt, following Wei et al.~\cite{wei2022}, cover chains,
    L-shapes, T-shapes, and edge placements. Crucially, no example includes
    $y$-coordinates; the planner operates entirely in the 2D horizontal
    plane.

  \item \textbf{Plan Verifier.} A rule-based post-planner validates the
    LLM-generated plan against the instruction text and $G_0$.
    Four deterministic correction passes run before execution:
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item \emph{Direction consistency:} compares directional words in the
        instruction against the plan's direction fields and flips
        contradictions.
      \item \emph{Endpoint cap correction:} when the instruction specifies
        placement at ``each end'' or ``both ends'' of a row, the system
        recomputes endpoints from the extension geometry and relocates
        cap-placement steps.
      \item \emph{T-shape extend correction:} detects T-shaped geometry in
        $G_0$, computes the stem's axis and growth direction, and replaces
        invalid extend directions.
      \item \emph{Stacking and count plausibility:} checks
        horizontal-versus-vertical intent and block-count consistency.
    \end{enumerate}
    These corrections operate on general geometric properties of the grid and
    instruction text. They do not reference specific evaluation scenarios and
    apply to arbitrary instructions and grid configurations.

  \item \textbf{Spatial Executor.} A deterministic engine processes each
    action $a_i$ on an in-memory representation of $G$, resolving relative
    references (``leftmost,'' ``the red one''), computing $y$ via
    Equation~\ref{eq:ystar}, and chaining positional context across steps.
    No LLM call is involved; all coordinate arithmetic is exact. A
    same-color skip-forward rule in the \texttt{extend\_row} handler detects
    when the start position is already occupied by a block of the same color
    and advances one grid step in the extension direction, preventing
    vertical stacking when horizontal extension is intended.

    Figure~\ref{fig:pdffigure} illustrates the 2.5-D decomposition. A plan
    specifying ``stack 3 red at $(5, 6)$, place red at $(6, 6)$, place red
    at $(7, 6)$, place blue at $(7, 4)$'' contains no $y$-coordinates. The
    executor computes a three-block column at $x\!=\!5$ (with $y = 0, 1, 2$),
    single blocks at $x\!=\!6$ and $x\!=\!7$ (both at $y\!=\!0$), and a blue
    block at $(7, 0, 4)$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{figures/25d_decomposition.pdf}
    \caption{2.5-D decomposition: the LLM planner generates 2D plans with
    $(x, z)$ coordinates and action types. The deterministic executor computes

    \label{fig:pdffigure}
\end{figure}

    In our initial approach, the LLM produced the complete 3D output directly,
    which required it to enumerate every block's $(x, y, z)$ coordinates and
    chain height calculations across stacking operations. That approach
    suffered from frequent off-by-one $y$-errors, duplicate placements, and
    miscounted heights. As shown in Section~\ref{sec:results}, the current
    approach eliminates these failure modes entirely.

  \item \textbf{Response Formatter.} Translates the final grid state into
    the protocol-required output format and validates the response before
    transmission.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Underspecification Handling}

Many BWIM instructions deliberately omit color or block count,
requiring the agent to decide between guessing and asking a clarification
question. The scoring function creates an asymmetric decision problem: a
correct build yields $+10$, an incorrect build yields $-10$, and each
question costs $-5$ regardless of the answer's correctness. Let $p$ denote
the probability of a correct guess. The expected values are:
\begin{align}
  \text{EV}_{\text{guess}}(p) &= 10p - 10(1 - p) = 20p - 10 \label{eq:ev_guess} \\
  \text{EV}_{\text{ask}} &= 10p_a - 10(1 - p_a) - 5 = 20p_a - 15 \label{eq:ev_ask}
\end{align}
where $p_a$ is the probability of a correct build after receiving the
answer. Setting Equations~\ref{eq:ev_guess} and~\ref{eq:ev_ask} equal and
assuming $p_a \approx 1$ yields the indifference threshold $p^* = 3/4
= 0.75$. Below this threshold, asking dominates guessing.
If the architect itself introduces errors (reducing $p_a$), the
threshold shifts but remains above 0.5 for realistic $p_a$ values.

\textbf{Missing color.} Heuristic analysis determines whether a color
can be inferred from context (e.g., reusing the sole color mentioned) or is
genuinely ambiguous. When ambiguous, the agent issues a clarification
question.

\textbf{Missing count.} Heuristic count inference (copying an adjacent
stack's height or defaulting to three) achieves approximately 65\% accuracy
on the BWIM instructions, well below $p^*$, so the agent asks whenever a
count is underspecified and a question has not already been used in the
current round.

A complication is that the architect answering questions is itself an LLM
that receives only the target structure as coordinates, not the original
instruction. Generic questions such as ``How many blocks should be in the
stack?'' produce a 23\% error rate because the architect cannot identify
which stack is referenced. We address this by generating
\emph{color-specific} questions that name the block color, allowing the
architect to identify the correct stack by counting blocks of that color in
its coordinate data. The answered count is patched directly into the
instruction text before the planner runs.

Because only one question is permitted per round, the agent prioritizes
color over count (color errors are harder to recover from heuristically).
When multiple phrases lack counts, the first uncounted phrase with a known
color is selected for the question. The remainder fall back to a three-level
heuristic cascade: copy the height of an adjacent stack, use the tallest
stack on the grid, or default to three (the modal count in the benchmark,
observed in 53\% of count-underspecified trials).

\section{Peephole Prompt Optimization}

Certain spatial concepts, including ``each end,'' chain references,
L-shape extensions, and T-shape junctions, are disproportionately
error-prone for GPT-4o-mini. These errors are not random: specific input
patterns reliably trigger specific failure modes. Our approach treats
these failures as analogous to suboptimal instruction sequences in compiled
code. In compiler design, a peephole optimizer scans a small window of
generated instructions, matches known suboptimal patterns, and replaces
them with more efficient sequences~\cite{mckeeman1965}. We apply the same
principle to prompts: the system scans each instruction against a library
of 15 pattern-matching rules, and when a rule fires, a targeted correction
with a worked example is injected into the prompt immediately before the
LLM call so that it falls within the model's local attention window.
Rules are independent and composable; multiple may fire on a single
instruction.

This methodology is domain-transferable and follows five steps: (1) run
systematic error analysis to identify recurring failure modes, (2) classify
failures by their input triggers, (3) write targeted micro-corrections with
worked examples for each failure class, (4) inject corrections dynamically
based on pattern matching against the input, and (5) validate that
corrections cause no regressions on inputs where the LLM already succeeds.
This process applies to any domain where LLMs make systematic,
input-predictable errors.

Table~\ref{tab:peephole} lists five representative rules from the set of
15. Each rule matches a trigger phrase, identifies the systematic error
the LLM produces for that phrase, and injects a targeted correction
with a coordinate example.

\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}p{1.8cm}p{3.2cm}p{3.2cm}@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Trigger} & \textbf{LLM failure mode} & \textbf{Injected correction} \\
\midrule
``in front of'' & Keeps same $z$, stacks vertically on the reference block & $z \mathbin{+}= 1$; $y$ resets to ground \\

To avoid overfitting to the evaluation set, all few-shot examples use
different colors, counts, positions, and phrasing from the benchmark
instructions. As a quantitative check, we measure unigram and bigram cosine
similarity between each enrichment example and every benchmark instruction.
The maximum similarity between any example and any benchmark instruction is
0.71, while the benchmark instructions reach pairwise similarities of up to
0.80 among themselves. The enrichment examples therefore share less surface
overlap with any individual evaluation item than the evaluation items share
with each other.

\section{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate on the BWIM benchmark's 160-round scenario, which pairs the
builder agent with both a rational architect (which answers questions
correctly) and an unreliable architect (which sometimes provides incorrect
information). All runs use the same codebase and differ only in model
selection or component ablation.

\textbf{Models.} The primary model is GPT-4o-mini (2024-07-18). We include
a comparison with GPT-4o (2024-08-06) to assess whether model scale
provides additional gains beyond the deterministic pipeline. All prompt
engineering and enrichment rules were developed against GPT-4o-mini.
The GPT-4o runs use the identical pipeline with no model-specific tuning.
We also evaluate NVIDIA Nemotron-3-Super-120B-A12B~\cite{nemotron2024},
an open-weight model quantized to NVFP4, served locally via
vLLM~\cite{vllm2023} on an NVIDIA Jetson Thor AGX Developer
Kit~\cite{jetsonthor} (14-core Arm Neoverse V3AE CPU, 2560-core Blackwell
GPU with fifth-generation Tensor Cores). No model-specific prompt tuning
was performed for the Nemotron runs.

\textbf{Metrics.} We report structural accuracy (percentage of rounds with
correct block placement) and the composite score (accuracy-weighted with
question penalties). For the final GPT-4o-mini configuration, we report
mean, standard deviation, and 95\% confidence interval across twelve
independent runs. For GPT-4o, we report the same statistics across six
independent runs.

\textbf{Sampling configuration.} The build planner uses temperature 0.1.
The fallback path and response formatter use temperature 0.2. The question
answerer uses temperature 0.2. Top-$p$ is left at the API default (1.0)
for all calls. No fixed random seed is set, so run-to-run variance
reflects model sampling non-determinism.

\textbf{Frozen codebase.} All reported runs use the same frozen code
commit. No code changes were made between runs within each model condition.
This ensures that variance reflects only LLM non-determinism, not
development changes.

\textbf{Additional implementation details.} The pipeline calls the LLM at
most twice per round (planning plus at most one clarification re-plan).
A dedicated pre-processing pass resolves chain references that span
multiple build steps. The agent tracks whether it has already asked a
question in the current round to prevent redundant queries.

configuration runs the full 160-round scenario $n = 7$ times on the same
frozen codebase. The full system baseline uses the $n = 12$ runs from
Table~\ref{tab:comparison}. Statistical significance is assessed with
Welch two-sample $t$-tests.

Removing 2.5-D decomposition, which reverts the system to direct 3D
coordinate generation by the LLM, produces the largest accuracy drop
($-50.7$ percentage points, $p < 0.0001$), reducing accuracy to 43.8\%.
This is comparable to the bare LLM baseline (40\% in early development),
confirming that 2.5-D decomposition is the foundational architectural
contribution. Underspecification questions contribute
the second largest effect ($-17.9$ pp), consistent with our expected value
analysis showing that asking is +EV when guess accuracy falls below 75\%.
Peephole prompt optimization accounts for $-11.3$ pp, and the skip-forward
rule for $-3.9$ pp. The plan verifier shows no statistically significant
effect ($-0.6$ pp, $p = 0.36$), suggesting that the planner rarely
produces the error patterns the verifier targets.

GPT-4o-mini outperforms GPT-4o by 4.3 percentage points with non-overlapping
95\% confidence intervals. The effect size (Cohen's $d = 6.8$) is very
large by conventional standards. This result is the opposite of what model
scaling alone would predict and supports the hypothesis that the
deterministic pipeline, not model capacity, is the primary performance
driver.

An important caveat is that all prompt engineering and enrichment rules
were developed against GPT-4o-mini due to budget constraints. GPT-4o-mini
token costs are roughly an order of magnitude lower than GPT-4o, which
made iterative development, error analysis, and prompt tuning across
hundreds of test rounds financially practical. The GPT-4o runs were
conducted only at the end of development as a comparison condition, using
the identical pipeline with no model-specific tuning. Prompt tuning and
performance optimization for a particular model does not necessarily
transfer to different models. Had development been conducted against
GPT-4o from the start, the GPT-4o results could plausibly be higher.
However, GPT-4o with the untuned pipeline still reaches
90.3\%, well above the best competing system's 76.3\%~\cite{hisandan} using GPT-4o
without a decomposition pipeline, indicating that the pipeline
architecture provides value independent of model-specific tuning.

GPT-4o also incurs substantially higher costs: input token pricing is
16.7$\times$ and output token pricing is 16.7$\times$ that of
GPT-4o-mini. Each 160-round GPT-4o-mini run completes in approximately
8.9 minutes (mean, $\sigma = 1.0$ min) at an estimated cost of
\$0.07 per run. GPT-4o runs take approximately 10.1 minutes (with a
1-second rate-limit throttle between API calls to avoid HTTP 429 errors)
at an estimated cost of \$1.10 per run, roughly 16$\times$ more expensive
for lower accuracy on this pipeline. The cost difference made
GPT-4o impractical as the development model for the iterative prompt
engineering workflow that produced the enrichment rules and verifier
corrections.

\subsection{Error Analysis}

Across all 12 frozen-commit GPT-4o-mini runs (1,920 total rounds), 104
rounds produce incorrect structures (5.4\% failure rate). We classify each
failure by comparing the expected structure, the builder's output, and any
architect responses to clarification questions.

Of the 104 failures, 57 (54.8\%) originate in the architect agent providing
incorrect information. The most common architect error is reporting the
wrong color when answering a clarification question (55 cases): for
example, the architect states ``Green'' when the target structure contains
Blue blocks, and the builder correctly builds with the (wrong) color it was
told. Two additional failures trace to incorrect count information from the
architect. The remaining 47 failures (45.2\%) are builder errors: 25 are
spatial reasoning mistakes (incorrect block positions despite correct
colors) and 22 involve incorrect color-position assignments in
fully-specified instructions.

If architect errors are excluded, the builder pipeline achieves 97.6\%
accuracy (1,873/1,920 rounds correct). This 3.0 percentage point gap
between raw accuracy (94.6\%) and builder-only accuracy (97.6\%) quantifies
the ceiling imposed by architect errors that the builder cannot
independently detect or correct.

\section{Limitations}

This study has several limitations that constrain the generality of our
findings.

\textbf{Single benchmark.} All results are reported on a single benchmark
(BWIM, 160 rounds). The 2.5-D decomposition approach depends on a
gravity constraint that makes vertical coordinates deterministically
computable. Tasks without such a constraint (e.g., free-form 3D
placement, assembly with rotations) would require a different
decomposition. Evaluating generalization to other spatial reasoning
benchmarks is an important direction for future work.

\textbf{Ablation variance.} Each ablation condition uses $n = 7$
runs, which provides strong power for large effects (the four significant
components all have $p < 0.0001$) but may miss smaller contributions.
The plan verifier result ($-0.6$ pp, $p = 0.36$) may reflect insufficient
power rather than a true null effect.

\textbf{Rule-based corrections and overfitting risk.} The 15 peephole
enrichment rules and four plan verifier corrections were developed by
analyzing failure modes on the BWIM instruction set. While the targeted
failure patterns (directional confusion, stack miscounting, L-shape and
T-shape geometry errors) are general spatial reasoning problems rather than
benchmark-specific artifacts, the specific trigger patterns and corrections
were tuned on this distribution. Their coverage of failure modes in other
instruction distributions is unknown. However, the competing team's lower
accuracy with GPT-4o (76.3\%) compared to our GPT-4o-mini result (94.6\%)
suggests that the pipeline design provides genuine structural benefit
beyond benchmark-specific tuning.

\textbf{Architect dependence.} The error analysis across all 12 runs shows
that 54.8\% of failures (57/104) originate in the architect agent providing
incorrect color or count information. Excluding these errors, the builder
pipeline achieves 97.6\% accuracy. The builder has no independent access
to the target structure and cannot verify architect responses, creating a
3.0 percentage point accuracy gap that builder-side improvements alone
cannot close.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

We presented a neuro-symbolic approach to LLM-based block construction in
which the LLM plans in a two-dimensional horizontal plane while a
deterministic executor computes all vertical placement via column occupancy.
On the BWIM benchmark, this 2.5-D decomposition enables GPT-4o-mini
to achieve 94.6\% mean structural accuracy ($n = 12$,
$\sigma = 0.72\%$), outperforming GPT-4o at 90.3\% ($n = 6$,
$p < 0.0001$) and confirming that the deterministic pipeline rather than
model capacity is the primary performance driver. A controlled ablation
study ($n = 7$ per condition) shows that 2.5-D decomposition accounts
for the largest single accuracy contribution ($-50.7$ pp when removed,
$p < 0.0001$), followed by underspecification questions ($-17.9$ pp),
peephole prompt optimization ($-11.3$ pp), and the skip-forward rule
($-3.9$ pp), all significant at $p < 0.0001$.

The broader principle is that LLM spatial reasoning can be made reliable by
identifying which dimensions of the output space are deterministically
computable from task constraints and removing those dimensions from the
LLM's responsibility. This principle extends to any domain where one or
more output dimensions are deterministic functions of the others, a
condition we term 2.5-D structure by analogy with Marr's 2.5-D
sketch~\cite{marr1982}.

The pipeline also transfers to edge hardware: a single Nemotron-3
120B run on a local NVIDIA Jetson Thor AGX achieved 95.0\% accuracy
(+960 score), within 0.4 percentage points of the GPT-4o-mini cloud
mean, with no prompt modifications and a median per-request latency
of 50.2 seconds.

Future work includes evaluating the approach on additional spatial
reasoning benchmarks, developing methods the builder can use to detect
architect errors, and investigating whether the 2.5-D decomposition
principle applies to tasks with more complex physical constraints such as
structural stability or connectivity requirements.
